Methods and Interpretation

This tool classifies endometrial carcinomas into four prognostic molecular subtypes following the WHO 2020 framework, FIGO 2023 molecular staging, and Swedish national guidelines (v3.2, 2026).

Classification Algorithm

The WHO 2020 hierarchical sequential classifier assigns one primary subtype in strict priority order. If a sample meets criteria for multiple subtypes, the highest-priority class is assigned and the others are reported as secondary features.

MMR / MSI Assessment

MMR deficiency is assessed from two independent sources:

1. MSI marker percentage (primary)

If MSI marker data is available (from the panel's microsatellite markers), the percentage of unstable sites is used directly:

  • MSI-H: ≥20% unstable markers (configurable threshold) → MMRd
  • MSI-L: >0% but <20% → not classified as MMRd
  • MSS: 0% → not classified as MMRd

2. MMR gene mutations (supporting)

Pathogenic variants in MLH1, MSH2, MSH6, PMS2, or EPCAM are evaluated for biallelic inactivation:

  • Biallelic (2+ hits in same gene): supports MMRd even if MSI markers are low
  • Single heterozygous: does NOT confirm MMRd — flagged as possible Lynch carrier, IHC recommended

Important note

Even with MSI marker data, IHC for MLH1/MSH2/MSH6/PMS2 is recommended for Lynch syndrome screening (identifies which protein is lost, guiding germline testing).

Truncating variants

Nonsense, frameshift, and splice-site mutations in TP53 are classified as pathogenic.

Exclusions

  • P72R/R72P: Benign polymorphism — excluded from classification.
  • Low-VAF variants: Subclonal variants below 5% VAF are flagged but not auto-classified.

Important caveat

TP53 sequencing and p53 IHC have ~92% concordance. This tool uses sequencing data as a surrogate. p53 IHC confirmation is recommended. In particular, TP53 deletions and loss of heterozygosity may show p53-null IHC pattern but appear wild-type by panel sequencing.

Limitations

  1. Research and clinical decision support only. Final molecular classification must be confirmed by a molecular pathologist in the context of histopathology and clinical information.
  2. MMR status from sequencing is less reliable than IHC. IHC for MLH1/MSH2/MSH6/PMS2 is the clinical standard for MMR assessment. Panel sequencing may miss promoter methylation (MLH1), large deletions, and other mechanisms of MMR loss.
  3. POLE VUS require secondary evidence. Variants of uncertain significance in the exonuclease domain are not auto-classified. TMB >100 mut/Mb and SBS10a/SBS10b signature support are needed.
  4. TP53 sequencing does not replace p53 IHC. Concordance is ~92%. Tumors with TP53 deletions or LOH may appear wild-type by sequencing but show aberrant (null pattern) p53 IHC. This is particularly relevant for serous carcinomas.
  5. Signature decomposition quality depends on variant count. Small panels (<300 genes) may yield unreliable signature fits. The tool uses substitution spectrum analysis (C>A, C>G fractions) as a more robust alternative for panel data.
  6. Multiple classifiers (3–6% of cases): Some tumors meet criteria for multiple subtypes. The hierarchical algorithm assigns one primary class, but all features are reported. Published evidence shows prognosis follows the highest-priority class (e.g., POLEmut+p53abn behaves like POLEmut).
